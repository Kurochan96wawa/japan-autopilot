Parents often worry that navigating crowded platforms like Tokyo's Shinjuku Station with a double stroller and a crying toddler will be an insurmountable barrier when they do not speak the local language. Finding public diaper-changing facilities, locating elevators, or managing a child's strict dietary needs can feel overwhelming when complex Kanji signs blur together. This comprehensive guide provides the exact Japanese phrases, cultural etiquette tips, and practical vocabulary parents need to navigate Japan smoothly and confidently with children of all ages.

  • Baby Facilities: Look for signs containing the word "Omutsu" (diaper) or use the phrase "Omutsu-kae dai wa arimasu ka?" to locate changing tables in major department stores like Isetan.
  • Dining with Kids: Request half-portions or child-friendly versions using "Kodomo-yo" and clarify common allergens like egg ("Tamago") or milk ("Gyuyu") before ordering.
  • Transit Rules: Children under 6 years old travel free on JR trains, while those aged 6 to 11 receive a 50% discount on transit cards registered with a passport.
  • Emergency Support: Locate local "Koban" (neighborhood police boxes) for immediate assistance, or dial 119 for medical emergency dispatch.

Finding Baby Care and Nursing Rooms

Navigating bustling shopping districts like Ginza or Shibuya requires knowing exactly where to find clean, quiet spaces for baby care. Most major department stores, such as Takashimaya, reserve entire floors for children's goods (usually the 6th or 9th floor depending on the branch) which feature incredibly clean baby lounges often designated as "Aka-chan Kyorei". To ask a store associate for the diaper changing station, use the phrase: "Omutsu kae dai wa doko desu ka?" (Where is the diaper changing table?). If you are nursing, ask: "Jyunyu-shitsu wa arimasu ka?" (Is there a nursing room?). In modern urban centers, parents can also keep an eye out for "Mamaro" pods—private, lockable mobile nursing booths measuring approximately 1.8 square meters that are increasingly found in shopping malls and transit hubs. When buying emergency baby supplies at stores like Akachan Honpo, you can ask for baby wipes by saying "Oshiri-fuki" or search for diapers using the term "Omutsu".

Ordering Food and Managing Dietary Restrictions

Dining out with children in Japan is highly rewarding, but communicating portion sizes and food allergies is critical. Many family restaurants, such as Saizeriya or Gusto, offer dedicated children's menus where a complete meal typically costs under 500 yen (as of 2026, confirm on the official site). To ask if a restaurant is child-friendly before entering, say: "Kodomo-tsure demo daijobu desu ka?" (Is it okay to bring children?). Once seated, you can request a high chair by asking: "Babii-chea wa arimasu ka?" (Do you have a baby chair?). If your child has a sensitive palate, ask if a dish is spicy: "Karai desu ka?" (Is it spicy?). For managing allergies, it is essential to present a printed allergy card or use clear spoken phrases. To state that your child cannot eat a specific ingredient, use the formula: "[Ingredient] arerugii ga arimasu." (My child has an [ingredient] allergy). For example, "Tamago arerugii ga arimasu" (egg allergy) or "Gyuyu arerugii ga arimasu" (milk allergy).

Essential Transit Phrases and Stroller Etiquette

The Japanese rail network is highly efficient, but boarding a crowded train requires quick thinking and specific vocabulary. If you are traveling with a stroller, look for the "Bebikaa-maaku" (stroller icon) painted on the platform floors of transit lines like the JR Yamanote Line, which indicates stroller-accessible train cars. To ask station staff for an elevator to avoid carrying a heavy stroller up stairs, ask: "Erebeetaa wa doko desu ka?" (Where is the elevator?). When purchasing tickets, remember that children under the age of 6 travel entirely for free on most trains, including the Shinkansen, provided they do not occupy an individual reserved seat. Children aged 6 to 11 require a child's ticket, which is priced at exactly 50% of the adult fare (as of 2026, confirm on the official site). To obtain a child-fare Suica or Pasmo IC card, visit a JR Ticket Office (Midori-no-Madoguchi) with your child's physical passport and tell the clerk: "Kodomo-yo Suica o onegai shimasu" (A child's Suica, please).

Emergency Scenarios and Medical Assistance

While Japan is exceptionally safe, minor illnesses or losing track of a child in a crowded space like Tokyo Disney Resort can happen to any family. If your child becomes separated from you, immediately locate a "Koban" (neighborhood police box) or a staff member and say: "Kodomo ga maigo ni narimashita" (My child is lost). For non-emergency health concerns, such as a sudden fever, you can locate a local pediatric clinic ("Shoni-ka") or visit a licensed pharmacy chain such as Matsumoto Kiyoshi or Welcia. To ask a pharmacist for children's medicine, say: "Kodomo-yo no kusuri wa arimasu ka?" (Do you have children's medicine?). In the rare event of a medical emergency requiring an ambulance, dial 119 immediately from any phone and say clearly: "Kyukyusha o onegai shimasu" (Please send an ambulance).

FAQ

How do I ask for tap water for my baby's bottle?

You can request clean tap water by saying "O-mizu o onegai shimasu" (Water, please) or ask for hot water to mix formula by saying "O-yu o onegai shimasu" (Hot water, please). Most convenience stores like FamilyMart will gladly assist if you ask politely.

What is the polite way to ask to fold a stroller on a bus?

While you are generally allowed to keep strollers open, crowded local buses in cities like Kyoto may require folding them. If a driver or passenger asks, they may say "Bebikaa o tatande kudasai" (Please fold the stroller). You can acknowledge this by saying "Hai, wakarimashita" (Yes, I understand).

How do I tell a restaurant server that my child cannot eat dairy?

Say "Nyuu-seihin arerugii ga arimasu" (My child has a dairy allergy). This ensures the kitchen staff understands to avoid butter, milk, and cheese in dishes like kids' curries or pasta.

Where do I dispose of dirty diapers when out in public?

Public trash cans are rare in Japan. You should carry a small, odor-blocking plastic bag with you. If you are inside a department store nursery room, look for the specialized diaper disposal bin labeled "Omutsu-yo gomi-bako". Never leave dirty diapers in standard convenience store trash bins.

What should I say if my teenager gets lost on the subway?

Teach your children to stay at the station and look for a station employee in a uniform. They can say "Eki-in-san, tasukete kudasai" (Station staff, please help me) or present a card with your contact details and the name of your hotel.

How do I ask if there is a child price for museum entry?

Before purchasing admission tickets at places like the Ghibli Museum or teamLab, ask: "Kodomo ryokin wa arimasu ka?" (Is there a child rate?). Children's museum tickets are often significantly discounted or free for toddlers under 3 or 4 years old (as of 2026, confirm on the official site).

Keeping a concise list of these practical phrases in your travel notes ensures that your family can confidently navigate transportation, dining, and daily logistics while enjoying the rich hospitality of Japan.
